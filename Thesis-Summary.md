# Thesis Summary

**Title:** Brain Signal Analysis During Sustained Attention
**Author:** Amin Behzadnia — MSc, Electronic Engineering (Islamic Azad University, Bojnourd Branch)

Attention is one of the most prominent cognitive functions, and studying it contributes not only to understanding healthy people but also to helping those with certain disorders. Better information about a person's attention helps researchers diagnose conditions such as attention disorders and attention deficits, and in turn find ways to address them.

This study analyses how the brain signals of **20 healthy university students (11 female, 9 male)** change while performing a sustained-attention task. The **Conjunctive Continuous Performance Task (CCPT)** was used to measure sustained attention, and brain signals were recorded with a standard **10–20 EEG cap with 19 active channels**. Signal power was analysed using the **Pwelch method in MATLAB**, with preprocessing in **EEGLAB** and statistical analysis in **SPSS**.

Based on the variance of their reaction times to a red-square target stimulus, participants were divided into two groups of 10 — **good** and **weak** performers.

**Key results:**
- Alpha-band power decreased when participants performed well on the CCPT and when their eyes were open compared with eyes closed (p < 0.001).
- Participants with weak performance showed higher relative Alpha and Delta power than those with good performance.
- A positive correlation was found between relative Alpha power and the standard deviation of reaction time at Pz.
- An increase in relative Alpha power during the CCPT at Cz increased the likelihood of missing the target.
- A negative correlation was found between the change in relative Beta power and participants' reaction time at Pz (p = 0.042, r = −0.45).

**Conclusion:** an increase in Beta power together with a decrease in Alpha and Delta power are favourable factors for strong performance on a sustained-attention task.

**Keywords:** Brain signals; Conjunctive Continuous Performance Task (CCPT); sustained attention.
